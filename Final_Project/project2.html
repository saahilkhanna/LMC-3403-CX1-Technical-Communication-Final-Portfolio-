Major Project 2

Framing Risk and Shaping Trust

Project Overview

For this group project, we analyzed how crisis communication from government health agencies can impact public trust and behavior. Our research focused on the CDC and FDA's communication during the Johnson & Johnson COVID-19 vaccine pause in April 2021, examining how their messaging about blood clot risks affected vaccine confidence across different demographics.

Working with my partner, Mokshith Mannuru, I contributed to all stages of the research process and helped create a poster and presentation that synthesized our findings effectively. Through this project, I saw how technical communicators can learn from real-world crisis communication to improve transparency and public understanding during health emergencies, and I developed stronger skills in visual design and information synthesis.

Key Highlights

Rhetorical Analysis

We examined actual CDC and FDA announcements before, during, and after the J&J vaccine pause. Our analysis revealed how language choices around "abundance of caution" and risk framing shaped public perception differently across demographic groups.

Data Visualization

We created charts showing public confidence levels across vaccine types (Pfizer, Moderna, J&J) and demographic breakdowns of how the pause affected willingness to vaccinate, using data from the KFF COVID-19 Vaccine Monitor.

Research Methodology

We analyzed both primary and secondary sources, official press releases, government statements, and media coverage, to understand public perception before, during, and after the vaccine announcements.

Key Findings

The CDC/FDA communication during the pause was transparent and responsive, but unfolded during an "infodemic" that amplified public fears. We recommend proactive misinformation combat as essential for maintaining trust during health crises.
